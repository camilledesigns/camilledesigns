Academy of Neuroscience for Architecture

A site designed to increase member enrollment, participation, and collaboration.

Overview
The Academy of Neuroscience for Architecture, an international research organization, was in need of a new site design. My team of 4 worked closely with the president of ANFA to create a mobile and desktop website design that encouraged membership enrollment and participation.
Role and Contributions
Lead UX Designer
Coordinated and led weekly client meetings
Conducted and analyzed up to 17 user interviews
Created user personas and style guide
Designed interactive lo-fi and hi-fi Figma wireframes

Defining the Challenge

The Academy of Neuroscience for Architecture (ANFA) is a growing research organization that is planning to expand membership opportunities and global outreach. The mission of the ANFA is to promote and advance knowledge that links neuroscience research to a growing understanding of human responses to the built environment. ANFA hosts a biennial conference for neuroscientists and architects to gather and share knowledge. Leaders of the organization want to create a bigger impact through hosting more events, facilitating more community building, and through a larger online presence.

Problem Statement

How might we further encourage the collaboration of neuroscientists and architects through a new website design?

Site Objectives

  • Promote research collaboration of neuroscientists and architects
  • Attract new members
  • Inform members about events and research opportunities
  • Advise university students of ANFA related classes and educational opportunities
  • Spread knowledge and research findings

Understanding Member Needs

User Interviews

ANFA’s current members are mainly composed of older (45+ years) architects and neuroscientists. Though the conference attracts researchers and architects of all ages (25 years - 60+ years). ANFA’s site needs to appeal to both an audience of ages between 25 to 70 and be accessible to less computer-savvy individuals.

After conducting 17 user interviews with ANFA members, we received a diverse range of feedback. We synthesized our data to create user personas and to find commonalities to identify the minimum viable product feature set.

Major Findings

  • Both architects and scientists feel undervalued in ANFA
  • No sense of community between members
  • Not enough communication between architects and scientists
  • Research grant information is extremely important

User Personas

To pin point user frustrations and needs, we created three personas. The primary personas are Joseph Lee, a neuroscientist, and Ben San, an architect. The secondary persona focuses on Lauren Diego, a neuroscientist who is new to ANFA and represents the younger generation of ANFA. These personas were a clear representation of our target audience and helped guide us through determining the MVP features.

MVP Features

Members and leaders of ANFA agree that channels of communication and community building are necessary to continue building the organization. The features below are required on the site so that it can fulfill the needs of ANFA.

  • Login and Register Pages
  • Ability to post and respond to articles, blogs, and research
  • Event listings page and information
  • Member directory with search and filter abilities
  • Register for conferences, submit abstracts, search through abstracts
  • Search through posted research

Beginning our Design

Tag Teaming the Site

Due to the size of the website, sections were divided amongst two teams. My team designed the Home page, Education section, Events section, About section, Sign In page, Register page, Page Not Found page, and footer. Both teams collaborated on the header/navigation bar design and the style guide.

My individual contributions:

  • UI Sketches: Events section, Education section, navigation menu, and footer
  • Low fidelity design of the mobile navigation menu
  • Low and high fidelity prototypes of the Education section, and desktop side menu design
  • Low and high fidelity event listing format in the Events section
  • Final design/high fidelity prototype of Page Not Found page

Adapting to our Client

After looking through the UI sketches, our client requested different pages for the Education section. Because the team member responsible for the section was absent at the meeting, we decided to switch sections. The client was very specific about what they wanted so it was important that the pages be designed by someone who understood the request.

Style Guide

Before building any wireframes, we produced a style guide. The style guide kept our design features consistent across all our prototypes. Our client requested that the color palette for the site be composed of “safe” colors and grey tones. To satisfy our client’s requests, we chose a dark grey as our primary color. Red is used as an accent color to give the site a pop of color and to direct users to clickable items. Because of their readability, Poppins and Open Sans, two sans serif fonts, were chosen for the typography. This is especially important for our older views and because the ANFA site will be a very text heavy site.

Center for Education

High-Fidelity Wireframes

For most pages of our low fidelity wireframes, we were given minimal feedback, such as telling what pictures to add or what small feature to edit. However, our client requested that I add a discussion forum and a recent event list to the Education Topic page and that I change the reading list to look similar to a directory.


While looking over the designs, we realized that the Page Not Found page was not helpful or informative. To make the page more useful to users, I added ANFA's mssion statement, conference information, and a site map. The mission statement would inform new users about the purpose of ANFA. In addition, according to our user interview data, most users go on ANFA's site for conference information. The site map gives an overview of the entire website, so users can see what pages are under each section.
